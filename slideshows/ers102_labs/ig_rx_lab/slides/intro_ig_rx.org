* Igneous Rock Definition 
** Igneous Rocks form from a liquid melt (magma).  
** Originate from fluids deep in the earth or for rocks that have been heated and **completely** melted

* Classification of Igneous Rocks
** Texture
*** Glass
*** Aphanitic
*** Phaneritic
*** Vesicular (foamy)
** Composition 
   * Light or Pink (Felsic)
   * Grey/salt and pepper
   * Dark/black or dark green (Mafic)

* Identifying Minerals
   In this lab, hand samples are evaluated using tests and
   observations. The most important are:
  * Cleavage: how many and angle between cleavage planes
  * Luster: metalic, glassy, etc.
  * Color: careful, some minerals come in a range of colors (eg: quartz)
  * Hardness: but how easy it is so scratch (not break)
  * Streak: color of powdered mineral, uses a porcelain plate (h = 6.5-7.5)

** ID minerals
   * Hardness tests done from hardest (glass) to softest
     (fingernail). If mineral scratched, *usually* stopped doing
     the other tests (if a mineral is harder than glass its harder
     than the other materials used for testing).
   * In some cases I verified unclear tests by reversing the test. For
     example, if a nail did not appear to scratch a mineral, I
     sometimes tested the mineral to see if it would scratch the nail.
   * If a mineral is harder than the streak plate, it can powder the
     streak plate (false response)
   * For the Igneous Rock Lab, you have the following minerals:
     hematite, quartz, hornblende, pyroxene, olivine, biotite,
     muscovite, orthoclase, plagioclase,
